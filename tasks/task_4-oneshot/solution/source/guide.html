A beginner's guide to live music

Going to a gig for the first time can feel like wandering into a private language. This guide walks you through the genres worth sampling, the venue types you'll encounter, and the small etiquette habits that make every show better — for you and everyone around you. No gatekeeping, just the practical stuff.

01 — Genres worth trying first

Six on-ramps into live music

Hand-picked for first-timers

Classical matinee

Daytime orchestral programmes built around shorter, friendlier repertoire.

Examples: Riverside Sinfonia · Northbank Strings · Hartwell Quartet

Gentle volume
Easy entry point Vivaldi at 3pm

Comfortable
Easy entry point Almost Fleetwood at The Pier

Acoustic singer-songwriter

One voice, one instrument, lots of stories between songs. Easy to follow.

Examples: Nora Halsey · Theo Bramwell · Yuki Okafor

Quiet rooms
Easy entry point Nora Halsey, Living Room Sessions

Jazz trio

Small ensembles, table seating, often with drinks service through the set.

Examples: Ellis Park Trio · Mona Vance Group · Blue Ivy Quartet

Indie rock

Guitars, hooks, mid-size rooms. The gateway genre most first gigs land in.

Examples: Pale Foxes · Lantern Year · Harbour & Vine

Loud, fun

Electronic / DJ set

No songs in the traditional sense — long flowing mixes, dancing expected.

Examples: Rivka Solis · Late Bloom · Octavo

Bring earplugs

02 — Venue types, compared

Where the show happens shapes the night

Three rooms, three feels

Intimate club

Sweaty, close, often standing. You can see the singer's setlist taped to the floor.

Capacity range
80 – 600 people
Typical ticket price
£12 – £28
Dress code
Whatever you wore today. Trainers are a yes.
Sightlines
Excellent if you arrive early; non-existent at the back. No assigned seats.
Great for beginners — low stakes, low cost

Theatre

Seated rows, a real stage, a curtain that actually rises. Best for listening.

Capacity range
600 – 2,400 people
Typical ticket price
£28 – £75
Dress code
Smart casual reads right; nobody will mind if you don't.
Sightlines
Numbered, predictable. Stalls beat balcony for most acts.
Ideal first venue — calm, comfortable, clear

Arena

The big one. Production-heavy spectacle, screens to help, queues for everything.

Capacity range
8,000 – 22,000 people
Typical ticket price
£60 – £180+
Dress code
Anything. Layers help — these rooms run hot then cold.
Sightlines
Variable. Tier 2 centre often beats expensive floor seats.
Doable, but pick a known act — it's a lot at once
03 — Etiquette FAQ

The unwritten rules, written down

8 questions
01 When do I clap?
At a rock or pop show, clap whenever you feel like it — between songs, during solos, after the first hit of a song you recognise. At classical and jazz shows, wait until the end of the full piece (not between movements). When in doubt, follow the room: if no one else is clapping yet, give it a beat.
02 Phones up or down?
Mostly down. A short clip of the opening song is fine. Filming the entire set blocks the people behind you and you'll never watch it back. Keep the screen at chest height, not above your head, and dim the brightness so it doesn't glow into the row behind.
03 Should I go anywhere near a mosh pit?
Only if you want to. Mosh pits usually open up in front of the stage at louder rock and punk shows — there's always a clear ring of space around them. Stand outside that ring and you're fine. If someone falls, the rule everywhere is: help them up immediately.
04 Can I talk during songs?
A whispered comment to the friend next to you is universal. Sustained conversation during a quiet acoustic ballad, a piano piece, or any seated theatre show is the single thing other audience members will resent. Save the catch-up for the bar between sets.
05 What's the deal with encores?
When the band walks off and the house lights stay down, that's not the end — it's the encore break. Keep clapping, maybe cheer, and they'll come back for two or three more songs. If the house lights come up, the show is properly over and it's safe to head out.
06 Merch lines: when to queue?
The fastest moment is right after doors open, before the opening act. The slowest moment is the instant the headliner walks off. Sizes always sell out from M outward, so go early if you care. Cash is rarely needed any more — most stands take contactless.
07 Photography — allowed?
At clubs and theatres, short phone photos are nearly always fine. Flash, never. Detachable-lens cameras are usually banned unless you have a press pass. Classical concerts and comedy shows are stricter — assume no photos unless a sign says otherwise.
08 Is it rude to leave early?
No. Trains stop running, kids need picking up, ears get tired — every venue knows. If you're seated, wait for a gap between songs to slip out so you're not stepping over knees during a quiet moment. From a standing crowd, just go.
04 — Mini glossary

Words you'll hear at the door

Eight terms, plain English
Support act
The opening band, on first. Usually 25–40 minutes. Often the find of the night.
Stage time
When the headliner actually starts. Doors open earlier — sometimes much earlier.
Standing / Stalls
Standing = no chairs, first come first served. Stalls = numbered ground-floor seats.
Soundcheck
The pre-show tune-up. You won't be in the room for it, but it's why doors run late.
Setlist
The running order of songs, taped to the stage floor. Roadies sometimes hand them out after.
Will-call
Tickets held at the box office, picked up with ID. Useful if your phone dies.
House lights
The audience lights. When they go down, the show's starting. When they come up, it's over.
Curfew
The hard end time the venue is licensed to. Why the encore is sometimes one song, not three.
